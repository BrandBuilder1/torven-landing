The Rat Experiment That Accidentally Solved Hair Loss

A Miami doctor was testing red light therapy on three different species. When rats, guinea pigs, AND chimpanzees all grew their hair back at Day 70 — he decided to try it on himself.

By the Editorial Team  ·  Dr. James Hanley, Miami FL  ·  May 2026  ·  8 min read

Dr. James Hanley wasn't trying to solve his own hair loss.

That wasn't the plan. He was a researcher. He had a lab, a protocol, and a very specific question he needed to answer: could red light therapy stimulate dormant hair follicles and trigger regrowth in mammals?

The experiment he designed would take 70 days. He expected mixed results. He expected variables. He expected the kind of inconclusive data that sends you back to the drawing board.

He did not expect what actually happened.

Three species. Three separate controlled groups. One protocol.

Each group was treated with the same red light therapy device at a consistent wavelength and frequency. Each group was documented from Day 1. Measured, photographed, and tracked with the same clinical rigour Dr. Hanley applied to all of his research.

By Day 70, all three groups had grown most of their hair back.

Not one species. Not two. All three — independently, consistently, across completely different biology, different coat types, different baselines — responding to the same red light protocol on the same timeline and producing the same result.

"When one species responds, you note it and keep going. When three completely different species respond at the same rate on the same timeline — that's not a coincidence. That's the mechanism working."

The Detail He Couldn't Ignore

The results across all three species were remarkable. But one detail kept pulling at Dr. Hanley's attention above everything else.

The chimpanzees.

98%
Shared Human DNA — Chimpanzees

Chimpanzees share 98% of human DNA. Their follicle biology is not merely similar to humans — it is nearly identical. The same cellular structures. The same growth cycle phases. The same dormancy triggers.

When red light therapy regrew the hair of rats and guinea pigs, that was compelling. When it produced the same result in chimpanzees — animals that are, genetically speaking, almost human — a very specific and unavoidable question formed in Dr. Hanley's mind.

He had been quietly watching his own hairline recede for the better part of a decade. Like most men, he had tried the usual things. Minoxidil. Specialised shampoos. Supplements that promised more than they delivered. Nothing had worked.

Now he was standing in his Miami lab looking at three species of animals with visibly fuller coats — one of which shared 98% of his DNA — holding a red light therapy device, and trying to talk himself out of the obvious question.

He couldn't.

"If it works on an animal that is 98% human — what is it going to do to an actual human scalp?"

— Dr. James Hanley
