A Miami Doctor Was About To Give Up On His Own Hair Loss. Then One Experiment Changed Everything Forever.

A Miami doctor was testing red light on three different animals. When all three began regrowing hair within the same week — he decided to try it on himself.

By the Editorial Team  ·  Dr. James Hanley, Miami FL  ·  May 2026  ·  9 min read

Dr. James Hanley wasn't trying to solve hair loss. He was a researcher with a lab, a protocol, and one simple question: what does red light actually do to hair follicles?

He gave himself 70 days to find out. He expected boring results — the kind that don't lead anywhere. He did not expect what happened on Day 70.

The Experiment Wasn't About Hair Loss — It Was About Something Else

Dr. Hanley was studying red light therapy — specifically, what a certain wavelength of red light does to living cells when you shine it on them consistently over time.

Hair follicles turned out to be a great thing to test this on. They're easy to track. They either grow hair or they don't. You can photograph them, measure them, and see clearly what's changed.

So he set up a clean experiment. Three groups of animals. Same red light. Same amount of time every day. 70 days. He started the timer.

He Used Three Completely Different Animals — On Purpose

One result from one type of animal doesn't prove much. The same result from three very different animals — that's much harder to explain away.

He chose rats (small, fast hair cycle), guinea pigs (different coat, different biology), and chimpanzees (almost the same DNA as humans). Three different species. Three different biologies. One identical red light protocol.

If all three responded the same way — that wasn't luck. That was the mechanism working.

By Day 70, All Three Had Grown Most Of Their Hair Back

The first 40 days were quiet. By Day 60, the treated animals were clearly different from the untreated ones — even people who knew nothing about the study could look at the photos and point to the right group.

By Day 70 — all three species had grown most of their hair back. Same result. Same timeline. Three completely different animals.

He checked the data three times looking for the mistake. There wasn't one.

The Chimpanzee Result Was The One That Stopped Everything

The rats impressed him. The guinea pigs confirmed it. But the chimpanzees were what kept him up at night.

Chimpanzees share 98% of human DNA. Their hair follicles work almost exactly like ours — same growth cycles, same hormones, same way they respond to damage. When something works in a chimpanzee, it's working in an animal that is nearly human.

When the chimpanzees grew their hair back the same way, on the same timeline — it was pointing to something that went far deeper than animal coat biology.

If it works in an animal that is 98% human... what would it do to an actual human scalp?

Day 1

Day 30
